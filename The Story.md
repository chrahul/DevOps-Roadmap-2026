
# A DevOps Interview From Years Ago Came Back to Me Last Week

*How a conversation with an unemployed engineer reminded me why most DevOps roadmaps on the internet are completely wrong.*

Yesterday evening I received a message on LinkedIn.

> *“Rahul sir, can I call you?”*

At first, I didn’t recognize the name.

But it sounded familiar.

Then after a few seconds, it clicked.

I had interviewed him a few years ago when I was working at Oracle.

We were hiring for a DevOps role. Like most interviews, I asked him questions about Linux, automation, CI/CD, and cloud infrastructure. His answers were not very strong. It was clear that he had read about DevOps, maybe watched a few tutorials, but he had never actually worked deeply with those systems.

Technically, the interview was already over.

He wasn’t ready for the role.

But before we ended the call, I told him something that I often tell many engineers during interviews.

I said,

> “Right now you don’t have the depth required for a DevOps role.
> But if you really want to build a career in this space, start with the fundamentals. Linux, automation, cloud concepts. Build things. Don’t just watch tutorials.”

He listened politely.

Like most candidates do.

Then we ended the interview.

And like thousands of other interviews, that conversation disappeared into memory.

Until yesterday.

---

## The Message That Brought Everything Back

When I replied to him, I asked a simple question.

“What happened?”

He said something that stayed with me.

> “Rahul sir, I lost my job.
> It has been more than six months.”

Six months.

Anyone who has experienced unemployment knows what those months feel like.

Every morning you open LinkedIn.

Every afternoon you check your email.

Every evening you ask yourself the same question.

What am I missing?

Anyone who has gone through that phase knows what those six months feel like.

The uncertainty.

The pressure.

The constant interviews.

The silent rejections.

He told me he was getting interview calls. In fact, he was even clearing the initial rounds. But in the final rounds, something was always going wrong.

He kept getting rejected.

And he couldn’t understand why.

Then he said something that made me pause.

> “You interviewed me once when you were at Oracle.
> My interview was not great. But you explained how I should prepare for DevOps.”

He continued.

> “At that time, I didn’t take it very seriously.”

There was no drama in his voice. Just honesty.

And maybe a little regret.

---

## The Part Most People Don’t Realize

Many engineers believe the biggest problem in their career is **lack of opportunities**.

But after interviewing hundreds of candidates over the years, I have noticed something different.

Most people don’t fail interviews because they are not intelligent.

They fail because their **knowledge is fragmented**.

They know a little Docker.

A little AWS.

A little Kubernetes.

A little Jenkins.

But when you ask them to explain how all these systems work together in a real production environment, the answers fall apart.

Not because they didn’t try.

But because the internet has made learning extremely confusing.

Everywhere you look there are videos saying:

“Become a DevOps engineer in 30 days.”

“Master Kubernetes in 15 days.”

“Learn Python in 10 hours.”

People who have never built a real production system are teaching others how to become experts.

And thousands of engineers are following these random roadmaps.

The result?

Six months later, they know many **tools**…

But they don’t understand **systems**.

---

## Our Two Hour Conversation

So yesterday evening we spoke.

Not for ten minutes.

Not for twenty minutes.

Almost **two hours**.

We didn’t talk about fancy tools.

We talked about something much simpler.

The **right order of learning**.

Because DevOps is not a collection of tools.

DevOps is a **layered system of skills**.

If the foundation is weak, everything built on top of it becomes unstable.

When we finished the conversation, he asked me something unexpected.

> “Sir, can I record this session?”

I asked him why.

He said something that I won’t forget.

> “Because this feels like a roadmap.
> A catalyst. A navigator for my career.”

That line stayed with me long after the call ended.

---

## Why I Decided to Write This

After the call, I kept thinking about something.

There are thousands of engineers out there who are in the **exact same situation**.

Talented.

Hard working.

But completely lost in the noise of random tutorials and unrealistic roadmaps.

Every day they are trying to learn something.

Watching another video.

Taking another course.

Saving another roadmap.

But deep inside they still feel uncertain.

They still feel like something is missing.

And recently another fear has started spreading everywhere.

**GenAI will replace engineers.**

People ask me this question frequently.

“Will AI take DevOps jobs?”

The honest answer is this.

I don’t know what the future will look like.

But I know one thing for sure.

If you build strong foundations and deep understanding, you can make yourself **unstoppable**.

The goal is not to become another engineer in the market.

The goal is to become the engineer companies cannot ignore.

---

## The Roadmap That Came Out of That Call

That two-hour conversation eventually turned into something much bigger.

I documented everything we discussed.

The exact sequence of skills.

The mistakes people make.

The right way to learn DevOps.

And I decided to share it publicly.

Because if it can help one engineer avoid six months of confusion, it is worth writing.

That conversation eventually turned into a roadmap.

Not a **“become DevOps engineer in 30 days”** roadmap.

But a realistic path that actually builds strong engineers.

In the next article, I will share the **exact learning sequence I explained to him**.

Not the one you see in random YouTube videos.

But the one that actually works in the real world.
