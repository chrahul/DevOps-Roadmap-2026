# DevOps Is Becoming a $25.5 Billion Industry by 2028 — And Companies Still Can’t Find Engineers

Something interesting is happening in the technology industry right now.

At the same time that AI is writing code, companies around the world are struggling to hire engineers who can actually **run and operate modern software systems**.

This contradiction is real.

On one side, developers are worried about AI replacing programming jobs. On the other side, organizations cannot find enough engineers who understand how software actually gets deployed, scaled, secured, and operated in production.

And that gap is exactly where **DevOps lives**.

---

If you look at the industry data, the signal becomes very clear.

The global DevOps market is projected to reach **about $25.5 billion by 2028**, according to multiple industry reports and market studies. Companies are investing heavily in automation, cloud infrastructure, CI/CD platforms, and software delivery systems.

But technology investment alone does not create value.

People do.

And that is where the real problem appears.

Several industry surveys show that **DevOps and DevSecOps remain among the largest skill gaps in modern IT teams**. A significant percentage of technology leaders say they struggle to find engineers with the right DevOps capabilities, and many DevOps positions remain open far longer than other engineering roles.

In other words, the industry is investing billions in DevOps transformation — but the talent supply has not caught up yet.

---

To understand why this is happening, you need to understand what DevOps really is.

Many people think DevOps is just a collection of tools.

Docker.
Kubernetes.
Terraform.
Jenkins.
Git pipelines.

But DevOps is not really about tools.

It is about **systems thinking**.

DevOps engineers understand how an application moves from a developer’s laptop all the way to a production system serving millions of users.

They design the pipelines that build software, test it, deploy it, scale it, monitor it, and keep it running reliably.

That responsibility sits right at the intersection of development and operations.

And that intersection is exactly why the skill set is so rare.

---

Traditional developers are trained to write application code.

But many developers never work deeply with infrastructure, networking, distributed systems, or production reliability.

System administrators, on the other hand, understand infrastructure very well.

But many of them never worked extensively with modern development workflows, automation pipelines, or containerized application environments.

DevOps sits directly in the middle of those two worlds.

To do the job well, you need to understand how applications are built, how infrastructure works, how systems scale, how automation removes bottlenecks, how security integrates into pipelines, and how to troubleshoot failures in complex environments.

It is a **broad engineering perspective**, and that perspective is still relatively rare.

Which is exactly why companies struggle to hire for these roles.

---

Another reason DevOps demand is rising so quickly is the pace at which modern software infrastructure is evolving.

Cloud adoption continues to accelerate across industries. Organizations that spent years running workloads on physical servers are migrating systems to cloud platforms.

Containers and Kubernetes have become the standard way modern applications run.

Infrastructure is increasingly defined using **Infrastructure as Code**, where entire environments are versioned, automated, and deployed through pipelines rather than configured manually.

Security is moving earlier into development pipelines through DevSecOps practices.

Each of these shifts requires engineers who understand automation, reliability, and system orchestration.

In other words, engineers who think like DevOps professionals.

---

Then AI entered the conversation.

Many developers worry that AI tools capable of generating code will eventually replace programming roles.

But there is another side to this story.

AI can write code snippets.

It cannot architect complex production systems.

It cannot reason about distributed system failures, deployment reliability, infrastructure design, or large-scale automation pipelines.

Those challenges require engineers who understand **how systems behave in the real world**.

DevOps engineers operate exactly at that level.

They design the infrastructure and automation systems that allow development teams to deliver software safely and reliably.

When production systems fail, when deployments break, or when applications become unstable, these are the engineers responsible for restoring stability.

And companies know how critical that responsibility is.

---

AI is also creating an entirely new layer of infrastructure complexity.

Machine learning systems do not stop at training a model. Once models are created, they need to be deployed, monitored, retrained, versioned, and integrated with real applications.

Data pipelines must run continuously. Training infrastructure must scale. Model behavior must be monitored.

This operational layer is what gave rise to **MLOps**, which is essentially DevOps practices applied to machine learning systems.

As organizations invest more in AI products and platforms, the need for engineers who can automate and operate these environments is growing rapidly.

In many ways, the rise of AI is **increasing the importance of DevOps rather than reducing it**.

---

Technology careers often move in cycles.

Ten to fifteen years ago, software engineering experienced a similar moment. Demand for developers was extremely high, and even basic programming skills were enough to enter the industry.

Eventually more people learned programming, educational programs expanded, and the hiring bar became higher.

DevOps today appears to be at an earlier stage of that cycle.

Demand is growing rapidly.

The market is expanding into a multi-billion-dollar industry.

Yet the number of engineers who truly understand the full DevOps ecosystem is still relatively limited.

This imbalance creates a window of opportunity.

A moment where learning these skills can position an engineer at the center of how modern software systems operate.

Because behind every cloud platform, every automated pipeline, every container platform, and every AI system, there is infrastructure that must be designed, automated, and maintained.

And the people who understand those systems are becoming some of the most valuable engineers in the technology industry today.
